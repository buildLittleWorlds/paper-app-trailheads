Session 9 · Reader path and argument

Order is an argument

The traveling question: in your world, what order do things have to come in — and what breaks when you shuffle them?
The bridge from class

In this week's Instructor Study page, you become a 1970 reader: Martin Gardner's column shows a little pattern, and the only way to know its fate is to compute the next generation yourself — so you click the cells you believe come next, and the page grades your squares against the real rule. Then five section cards — hook, what to do, the interaction, what to notice, next question — become movable, and every arrangement re-renders the page live. Explanation-first buries the button under three paragraphs; payoff-first spoils the reveal before you've acted.

That's what this page sends into your territory: order is an argument. Everything you love — jokes, songs, recipes, levels — argues in a particular order, and shuffling it changes what it says.

Pick a lane, or wander

Trailheads

Every card is a question you could chase. Try it is the smallest version — buildable tonight. Where it could go is the same question weeks deeper: branches to explore once the small version works (tags name the session that hands you the tool). Two prompts to copy: try-it builds the small version now; branch has the AI interview you and propose your version before writing any code. Chips mark the ladder: wonder → watch → compare. Your app is its own lane — those cards do not hand you a curiosity, they send you back into something you already built to go looking for one.

The order bench

The Bench

Every card above rearranges somebody else's thing. This one rearranges yours. Your page argues in an order you probably never chose on purpose. Take inventory, move one piece, and predict the first ten seconds before you test them.

2 Take inventory of your page as it is right now. No judging yet — just look.

Fill in any that apply. Each one becomes a piece you could move.

3 Pick the one piece you'll move, and guess.

With that moved, in their first ten seconds a stranger would — but I don't actually know.

Then run the sound-off test: hand it over, say nothing for ten seconds, watch the cursor. Keep the new order only if the stranger acts sooner.


      


      
Not sure what's first? Open your page on your phone, count three seconds, close it. Whatever you remember — that was first. And that sentence you always say out loud during demos? It's probably a missing label, not a speech.
