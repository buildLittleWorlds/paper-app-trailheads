Session 3 · Attention and context

What counts as context?

The traveling question: what does a thing — a note, a move, a word, a bark — need to know before its meaning settles?
The bridge from class

In this week's Instructor Study page, the word "bank" glows river in one sentence and money in another — the same word, two minds, settled entirely by its neighbors. Swap one word (reply → knife) and the whole reading flips. A fixed ring always sees the same eight neighbors; a spotlight chooses which faraway words matter, and by how much.

That's what this page sends into your territory: meaning isn't in a thing alone — it's settled by what's around it. Every lane below has a "same thing, different surroundings" waiting to be built.

      Honesty note. Every card on this page was authored by your instructor; the Dealer's shuffles happen randomly in your browser; the copy buttons write to your clipboard and nowhere else. Nothing you click is recorded, collected, or sent anywhere. And one rule travels with every analogy here, the same one the course keeps for cellular automata and transformers: analogies find questions, not answers. A chord is not attention; a chat app is not a transformer. Real attention weighs many words at once, through learned math, in ways a note-muting toy only gestures at. The idea travels; the mechanism doesn't.
